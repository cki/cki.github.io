A case for why tankers might still be undervalued

2020-04-24

Disclaimer: I am not a professional investor. I am a retail investor. Fintwit would probably call me a tanker tourist. Do not take anything I write as investment advise. Do not trust my numbers. Caveat emptor.

Earnings season is coming up. I wanted to take the time and check in on my investments. In the following I will present one tanker thesis and one lazy model of how to evaluate tankers. We will sum average Spot TCE and divide by Market cap.

Thesis

I started to invest in Tankers in late 2019. While the thesis has not changed since then, it got boosted.
The initial thesis was: The crude and product tankers market has gotten constraint. Supply and demand is shifting.

In late 2019 IMO2020 was on the horizon forcing all tankers to use low sulfur oil or invest in scrubbers in 2020.
IMO2030 with yet unknown looming conditions is constraining the tanker order books for newbuilds.
Who is going to buy a vessel that gets written off in 20 years if they don't know what specs the vessel should have? With an aging fleet supply and demand is going to shift in favor of suppliers.

COVID-19 added a boost to the thesis.The increasing contango in the oil market is taking up vessels for storage deals, decreasing the supply of tankers even further and putting a bottom under tanker rates.

Counterthesis

Management is not what you wish for. When checking up on corporate management you are reminded of pirates of the old age. If they decide to waste the companies inflow of cash (on newbuilds) the whole thesis could crumble.

Volatility is high. It seems that some algos have bunched tanker companies with oil companies. This makes for a very rough ride when investing in tankers. It however also gives quite decent entry points into a possible position.

Tanker customers could default. Tanker companies apparently can not take posession of product legally. They still have to deliver the cargo. They try to diversify their customers but given that their background is not diverse you have systemic risk in your portfolio. When a customer defaults the vessel might be hostage to the bankruptcy management of the customer. Where do they offload their cargo given current storage dynamics? Tanker payment seems to be subordinated debt.

The stonks might not move. I honestly do not know why you invest if you do not believe in buying a undervalued company given high earnings.
Some companies have dividend "guaranteed" policies so there might be a downside protection.

My calculations

MCAP => Marketcap of a given company
Mean - σ => Mean TCE Spot rate for a given vessel type minus one standard deviation
Mean => Mean TCE Spot rate for a given vessel type
Mean + σ => Mean TCE Spot rate for a given vessel type plus one standard deviation

Are tanker companies still cheap?

In the following I take the number of vessels a company has in a given vessel type. For every given vesseltype I take aboves rates and multiply by the number of days since the start of the year. To take matters into perspective I divide the endproduct by the Marketcap.
DHT's calculation would look like this: (98.9 * 27 * 114 * 1000) / 1190000000

This methodology has many flaws!
Not all vessels of a given company have spot exposure. Keep that in mind.
I do not have TCE for all vesseltypes. If a vessel falls into a category for which I do not have data, it earns nothing.
Some vessels are bunched together on companywebsites.
Product tankers could take crude. I have no source on in which market a tanker operates.
For some of those flaws I tried to add a note.

Given aboves tables and a companies balancesheet I guess you can decide for yourself: Downgrade the summed TCE / MCAP by what you would be the correct spot exposure for the company. Downgrade some more because of the blody pirates in managment that will take their pound of blood. Open the balancesheet and check the guestimated cash flows against their liabilities.

Please also note that the upside of tanker stocks currently is somewhat tied to the upside of a contango in the oil market. The original thesis however remains: The fleet is aging and that might not change for a while.
